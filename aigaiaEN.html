Incident Report: Website Outage – www.aigaia.com.cy

1. Incident Overview

Date: 19 January 2026
Timezone: Cyprus (EET)
Affected Service: www.aigaia.com.cy
Hosting Model: Managed hosting (third-party provider)

On 19 January 2026, the website www.aigaia.com.cy experienced an unplanned service outage during business hours. The issue was first identified internally by staff and not by the hosting provider. Service was restored approximately two hours later.

Post-incident technical analysis indicates that the outage was most likely caused by a reactive DNS or upstream routing correction, rather than a client-side DNS cache issue as initially communicated by the hosting provider.

---

2. Timeline of Events

  • ~10:00 – Staff report inability to access www.aigaia.com.cy
  • 10:00–11:05 – Hosting provider notified; no proactive alert from provider monitoring
  • 11:05 – Provider attributes issue to local DNS cache; attempts remote DNS flush on a workstation (unsuccessful due to permissions)
  • 11:30 – Provider claims issue has been fixed
  • 12:03 – Website becomes accessible internally and externally
  • Post-incident – DNS, TLS, and HTTP-layer diagnostics performed
---

3. Technical Findings

3.1 DNS Resolution

  • A record resolves globally to 181.214.31.81
  • Observed TTL post-incident: ~5 minutes
  • No AAAA (IPv6) record present
  • DNS resolution currently consistent across all regions

Interpretation:
A very low TTL is characteristic of a recent or reactive DNS change, rather than a long-standing stable configuration. This aligns with emergency troubleshooting or rollback activity.

---

3.2 TLS / SSL Configuration

  • Certificate valid from: 10 December 2025
  • Issuer: Let’s Encrypt (R12)
  • No certificate reissue or renewal on the incident date
  • TLS configuration rated Grade A

Interpretation:
The hosting endpoint and application stack existed prior to the incident. The outage was not caused by a new deployment, server rebuild, or TLS reconfiguration.

---

3.3 HTTP / Application Layer Verification

A post-recovery HTTP header inspection was performed using: 

curl -I https://aigaia.com.cy

Observed response:

HTTP/2 301
server: LiteSpeed
x-powered-by: PHP/5.6.40
x-redirect-by: WordPress
location: https://www.aigaia.com.cy/

Interpretation:
This confirms that:

  • The web server (LiteSpeed) was operational
  • PHP was executing successfully
  • WordPress was running and issuing a normal canonical redirect

This conclusively rules out:

  • Application failure
  • Web server downtime
  • Broken virtual host configuration
---

4. Root Cause Assessment

Most likely cause:

A reactive DNS A-record change or upstream routing/backend correction performed after the outage began, pointing traffic back to an already running server.

Causes not supported by evidence:

  • Client-side DNS cache failure
  • IPv6 misconfiguration
  • TLS or certificate issues
  • Application or server crash
---

5. Service Management & Process Failures

Regardless of whether the change was reactive or planned, the following deficiencies were identified:

  • No proactive monitoring: The provider did not detect or alert on the outage
  • Incorrect initial diagnosis: Client-side DNS cache blamed without evidence
  • Lack of communication: No incident notification or updates
  • No post-incident reporting: No written RCA or timeline provided
  • Legacy environment indicators: PHP 5.6 (end-of-life) suggests weak operational hygiene
---

6. Impact

  • Loss of website availability during business hours
  • Operational disruption
  • Reputational risk
  • Internal time spent on investigation and escalation
---

7. Recommendations

  • Implement provider-side external uptime monitoring with alerting
  • Define and enforce a formal SLA with uptime and response guarantees
  • Require advance notice for planned changes and post-incident reports for outages
  • Review suitability of current hosting provider for critical services
---

8. Conclusion

The outage of www.aigaia.com.cy on 19 January 2026 was resolved through a reactive technical correction. However, the absence of proactive monitoring, incorrect diagnosis, and lack of formal incident handling represent a broader service delivery failure inconsistent with managed hosting best practices.

Corrective actions and contractual clarification are required to restore confidence in the service.
